Cursor and agents at a glance

If you only read one subsection for tooling, make it this one. Everything below goes deeper; this is the crib sheet for recruiters, professors, and collaborators.

Cursor (what it is for me)

Cursor is my primary code editor: a fork of VS Code-style workflows with built-in AI—chat, inline completions, and project-wide context from the repo.

  • Repo context: I point the model at real paths, @file / @folder style references, and a checked-in .cursorrules file so the same “product requirements” apply every session.
  • Models: I pick the tier that matches the task (quick lint vs. long reasoning); see tips below for budget and quality tradeoffs.
  • Not autopilot: I still run git, read diffs, and revert when the suggestion is wrong—authorship and accountability stay with me for grading, employment, and client-facing work.

Agents (what “agentic” means here)

In Cursor, an agent is a mode where the tool can plan, call tools, edit multiple files, and iterate toward a goal while I supervise—more autonomy than a single inline suggestion, less than unsupervised production deploys.

  • Typical loop: I describe the outcome → the agent proposes steps → I approve or correct → it edits → I review the full diff before commit or submit.
  • Optional extras: When a workspace enables them, MCP (Model Context Protocol) hooks can connect other tools; I only rely on what I understand and can audit.
  • Red lines: No agent crosses academic integrity, PHI / HIPAA-shaped boundaries, client confidentiality, or accessibility sign-off without explicit human gates—see research, SWOT, and business–tech–law sections.

Market context: the wave moment (iPhone, Facebook, Amazon, Tesla)

Every few years a technology shows up and the room splits: some people say it will fix everything, some say it will ruin everything, and most people are just trying to figure out what the words mean. That is where a lot of us are with generative AI and agentic tools right now. I am naming that on purpose. It is normal to feel behind, skeptical, or over-hyped all in the same week.

The pattern from earlier waves is always the same: the product gets easier, the business model gets clearer, and law, regulation, and private ordering catch up late—often after liability stories and consent fights make the news. Phones and social feeds rewired attention and privacy defaults. Commerce platforms rewired trust, logistics, and consumer protection fights. Mobility rewired infrastructure subsidies and safety rulemaking. Generative AI is rewiring IP, employment, and professional responsibility arguments in real time. My job is not to pretend that is neutral. It is to stay transparent, keep humans accountable, and use the tools where they help—without misrepresenting provenance, data handling, or institutional rules.

Business, technology, and law: how I triangulate

The same business–technology–law triangle shows up in every serious project: what the business promised, what the systems actually do, and what law, contract, or policy says you may, must, or must not do. I stay in that triangle as a student and builder—reading the stack the way strategy and compliance-adjacent coursework trained me to read a market or a roadmap.

  • Commercial and vendor layer: ToS, acceptable use, and AI vendors’ data processing choices matter. I assume subprocessors exist until I read otherwise, and I treat pasting sensitive content into a cloud chat as a data-handling decision—not a neutral keystroke.
  • Institutional “private law”: Syllabi, honor codes, IRB-style boundaries, and employer or client policies are the hard constraints. They beat marketing copy from any platform.
  • Sector norms and public law hooks: HIPAA-shaped research taught me minimum necessary and accountability language. WCAG work for cities and nonprofits often sits next to Section 508-style obligations or settlement DNA. Studying in the EU sharpens how I read GDPR headlines and digital rulemaking as part of an environmental scan—same as any other strategic PEST input.
  • IP and attribution: Generative output is a draft layer until a human verifies, edits, and takes responsibility. That protects integrity and reduces downstream IP and misrepresentation risk in anything that carries my name or a client’s.

Plain names: AI, copilots, and “agentic” workflows

Here is the vocabulary I use when someone asks me at a family dinner or in a lab meeting.

  • AI (here) usually means a large language model or similar system trained to predict plausible next words or actions from data. It can draft, summarize, and suggest. It is not a conscience and it is not automatically right.
  • Copilot-style help is the inline suggestion: autocomplete, chat next to your file, quick rewrites. You still click save. You still own the diff.
  • Agentic means the software can chain steps: read context, call tools (search the repo, edit files, run a command), and loop until a goal is met, with you supervising. More autonomy in the loop, more responsibility on the human to verify.

What an “agent” does in my editor (still plain English)

In Cursor, an “agent” is software that can take a goal, use tools (search your repo, edit files, run commands), and iterate, always under human supervision. It is not magic and it is not a replacement for judgment. It is acceleration with guardrails. I use agents like a very fast pair-programmer who still needs a lead: I set direction, I read the diff, I revert when it goes sideways.

Where my research fits (legal and ethical constraints as requirements)

My recent lane is health informatics and ethical, human-in-the-loop AI (IU Luddy Proactive Health and ServeIT). Those are regulated-adjacent products: non-functional requirements read like a compliance backlog—HIPAA awareness, auditability, breach-sensitive handling, and stakeholder trust. Agents can accelerate drafts or refactors; they are not a substitute for compliance judgment, privilege- or confidentiality-aware review, accessibility sign-off, or a named human who owns the outcome under policy and law.

The part people don’t post on LinkedIn

Agents hallucinate, skip steps, and sound sure when they are wrong. I revert commits. I send rambling prompts when I’m tired and then wonder why the output wandered. The win isn’t looking flawless. It’s catching problems early, saying “I checked this file,” and knowing what you did not let the tool touch.

Golden retriever energy (seriously)

I’d rather be embarrassingly honest than mysteriously perfect. Ask questions early, document the boring parts, celebrate the team, and assume good intent. We don’t have to be afraid of AI. We have to be clear about when and how we use it. The future belongs to people who can say “here’s my stack, here’s my limit, here’s how you can work with me.”

What IU Luddy and the UCM course monorepo reinforced

This is not a lecture. It is the operating model that stuck from informatics, Kelley-side business and strategy exposure (contracts, markets, and governance language), EU institutions and policy coursework, health and ServeIT research, and from maintaining one repo with five parallel products (course tracks): product-management, introduction-to-eu, management-global-markets, strategic-management, spanish-for-beginners, plus a single source of truth in docs/INDEX.md.

  • Human-in-the-loop is a requirement, not a slogan. If a deliverable touches patients, nonprofits, or grades, the model is a draft subsystem. A named human still validates, documents, and can veto ship.
  • Environmental scan before you bet the roadmap. Same instinct as PEST and SWOT in strategy coursework: political, economic, socio-cultural, technological forces, plus legal and regulatory moves. What is tailwind? What is enforcement or reputational risk? Who is the real buyer, approver, or data controller?
  • Traceability beats narrative. Just like week folders must match the index, claims need receipts. Agents fail hardest when they invent citations, paths, or policies.
  • Right-size the integration depth. Shallow assist for low-risk edits; deeper agent loops only when the acceptance criteria and rollback path are clear.

SWOT analysis: agentic AI in my workflow (individual, not company-wide)

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) is the same four-box tool from strategy and product classes, applied here as a personal risk register for how I use agents in school, research, and this portfolio. Treat it like a living doc: your quadrants should change as vendors and syllabi change. Copy the method, not my bullets.

Strengths

  • Faster time-to-first-draft on outlines, specs, comments, and repetitive edits.
  • Repo-aware tools reduce defects (cross-file typos) when fatigue hits.
  • Shorter path from blank page to a reviewable artifact I can accept or reject.
  • Brainstorming copy when the facts are already validated elsewhere.

Weaknesses

  • Hallucinations packaged as confident answers (quality risk).
  • Knowledge gaps: training cutoffs miss your syllabus, employer stack, or locale.
  • Operational cost: metered plans and long threads eat time if work is not batched.
  • Data handling: pasting PHI, grades, trade secrets, or privileged content into a vendor cloud is a breach or integrity incident waiting to happen—usually faster than any cleanup sprint can unwind.

Opportunities

  • Reallocate hours to verification, accessibility testing, and stakeholder interviews.
  • Cleaner runbooks and READMEs if the model stays a formatter, not an oracle.
  • Enablement: shared prompts and disclosed workflows for teams and classmates.
  • Governance alignment with NIST AI RMF-style language for audits, procurement questionnaires, and course policies.

Threats

  • Integrity and brand risk if disclosure does not match institutional rules or misrepresents authorship.
  • Contract and employment exposure when tool use violates client policy, confidentiality clauses, or work-product rules.
  • Capability atrophy when people ship without understanding the diff.
  • Regulatory or ethical exposure in health and nonprofit delivery without human sign-off.
  • Roadmap pressure from hype: “AI-first” mandates that skip discovery, QA, and recordkeeping.

Tips and tricks that keep me sane (and budgets happier)

  • Write repo rules once. A small .cursorrules (or project rules) saves you from repeating the same lecture to the model.
  • Anchor on real files. Use @file or paste paths so the model grounds in what actually exists, not what it imagines.
  • Batch small tasks. One message with three related edits beats three long threads.
  • Always read the diff. Agents are fast; you’re the editor-in-chief.
  • Prefer the right-sized model. Quick lint fixes don’t need the heaviest tier every time. That saves usage if your plan is metered. When I need real reasoning, I gravitate toward Claude or Gemini in Cursor; when the task is tiny, I downshift so I don’t burn the budget.
  • When stakes are high (grades, HIPAA, production), slow down. Type a short checklist the output must satisfy before you merge or submit.
  • Your syllabus and honor code win. If your school wants disclosure on assignments, do it. No tool is worth an integrity mess.
  • Rubber-duck the task first. If you can’t explain the job in two sentences, the agent probably can’t execute it cleanly either.
  • Disclose when it matters. For class, work, or portfolio pieces, follow your institution’s policy. Default bias: honesty ages better than vibes.
  • Read the boring pages. When a tool touches client or research data, skim ToS, privacy policy, and (if offered) DPA or enterprise terms for retention, training use, and subprocessors—then behave accordingly.
