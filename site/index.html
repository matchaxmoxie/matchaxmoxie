How I actually work (Cursor, agents, and research)

Yes, I use Cursor. Yes, I use AI agents. I’m not going to pretend this site (or my notes, or a lot of my code drafts) materialized without modern tooling. What matters is that I stay in the loop: I review, I edit, I decide what ships, and I own the mistakes. Transparency isn’t shameful. It’s how we build trust while the tech is still new to most people.

What people mean by “agents” (plain English)

In practice, an “agent” is software that can take a goal, use tools (search your repo, edit files, run commands), and iterate, always under human supervision. It’s not magic and it’s not a replacement for judgment; it’s acceleration with guardrails. I use them the same way I’d use a very fast pair-programmer who still needs a lead.

Where my research fits

My recent lane is health informatics and ethical, human-in-the-loop AI (IU Luddy Proactive Health and ServeIT): systems that touch real patients and nonprofits have to be explainable, careful, and tested. Agents can help draft or refactor; they don’t replace HIPAA sense, stakeholder trust, or accessibility review.

The part people don’t post on LinkedIn

Agents hallucinate, skip steps, and sound sure when they are wrong. I revert commits. I send rambling prompts when I’m tired and then wonder why the output wandered. The win isn’t looking flawless. It’s catching problems early, saying “I checked this file,” and knowing what you did not let the tool touch.

Golden retriever energy (seriously)

I’d rather be embarrassingly honest than mysteriously perfect. Ask questions early, document the boring parts, celebrate the team, and assume good intent. We don’t have to be afraid of AI. We have to be clear about when and how we use it. The future belongs to people who can say “here’s my stack, here’s my limit, here’s how you can work with me.”

Tips and tricks that keep me sane (and budgets happier)

  • Write repo rules once. A small .cursorrules (or project rules) saves you from repeating the same lecture to the model.
  • Anchor on real files. Use @file or paste paths so the model grounds in what actually exists, not what it imagines.
  • Batch small tasks. One message with three related edits beats three long threads.
  • Always read the diff. Agents are fast; you’re the editor-in-chief.
  • Prefer the right-sized model. Quick lint fixes don’t need the heaviest tier every time. That saves usage if your plan is metered. When I need real reasoning, I gravitate toward Claude or Gemini in Cursor; when the task is tiny, I downshift so I don’t burn the budget.
  • When stakes are high (grades, HIPAA, production), slow down. Type a short checklist the output must satisfy before you merge or submit.
  • Your syllabus and honor code win. If your school wants disclosure on assignments, do it. No tool is worth an integrity mess.
  • Rubber-duck the task first. If you can’t explain the job in two sentences, the agent probably can’t execute it cleanly either.
  • Disclose when it matters. For class, work, or portfolio pieces, follow your institution’s policy. Default bias: honesty ages better than vibes.
